## Summary

A framework video arguing that profitable trading requires three pillars — edge, psychology, and risk — working together, not in isolation. The presenter (Mulham) distills 8 years of experience into the claim that strategy-hopping is a symptom of not having this foundation, and that expectancy math is the only honest arbiter of whether a system has an edge.

## Strategies

- **Three-Pillar Trading System:** Any valid trade requires (1) direction — which way is price most likely to move; (2) key level — where is price coming from and going to; (3) entry — precise enough that the math works in your favor. If you can't answer all three before entering, you don't have a trade.

- **High Probability Range (Weakness → Strength formula):** A zone where continuation is more likely than reversal. Identified by the sequence: price fails to close beyond a level (weakness) followed by a strong break of structure in the opposite direction (strength). The range defined between these two events is the high-probability zone; entries are taken on retracement back into that range.

  - Weakness = fake out / failure to displace (price attempts a break, fails to close)
  - Strength = strong close / break of structure confirming directional bias
  - Entry = on retracement back into the W→S range

## Metrics & Ratios

- **Expectancy formula:** `(Win Rate × Avg Win) - (Loss Rate × Avg Loss)`. Positive = profitable over a large sample. The presenter explicitly states 30% win rate + 3:1 R:R = positive expectancy; 40% win rate + 1:1 R:R = negative expectancy.

- **Break-even win rate by R:R:** At 1:1 R:R, need 50% win rate to break even. At 2:1+, the required win rate drops below 50%. The asymmetry means higher R:R gives more room to be wrong.

- **Drawdown recovery math:**
  - 25% drawdown → need 33% gain to recover
  - 50% drawdown → need 100% to recover
  - 75% drawdown → need 300% to recover
  - Implication: capital preservation is mathematically more important than return maximization.

- **Equity curve health:** Healthy curve = many trades, consistent position sizing, gradual upward slope with normal variance. Unhealthy curve = sharp spikes up/down, inconsistent sizing, potential single-trade blowouts.

## Risk Management

- The only variable a trader fully controls is position size / risk per trade. Everything else is probabilistic.
- Protect the base capital that compounding works from. Drawdown asymmetry makes losses disproportionately expensive to recover.
- Edge only manifests across a large sample — an edge cannot be validated or invalidated from 10 trades. Must stay in the game long enough for statistics to play out, which requires consistent small risk per trade.
- Fixed fractional position sizing (risk the same amount every trade) is implied by the healthy equity curve description.

## Journal / Process

- **Strategy-hopping loop:** Strategy → excitement → loss streak → new strategy → repeat. Root cause is not having the three-pillar foundation, not a bad strategy. Recognition prompt: "Am I changing strategy because of a structural flaw in the edge, or because of a losing streak within a valid edge?"

- **Psychology root cause framing:** FOMO, overtrading, revenge trading, moving stops are all symptoms. The root is the nervous system treating financial threat as physical threat (same stress response). "Fixing discipline" directly doesn't work — it treats symptoms. The fix is nervous system regulation / reducing the emotional charge of each trade (implicitly: smaller size, rules clarity).

- **The dangerous win:** A trade won while breaking rules is more damaging than a loss taken with broken rules, because it gives the brain evidence that rule-breaking works. This reinforcement can compound into larger rule violations at worse moments. Journaling prompt: "Did I follow process on this trade, regardless of outcome? Would I take this exact trade again?"

- **Process > Outcome:** A losing trade executed perfectly is a good trade. A winning trade executed with rule breaks is a bad trade. Grade trades on process adherence, not P&L.
